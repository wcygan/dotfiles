# Culinary Travel Guide

Food is the fastest, most democratic gateway to culture. You don't need to speak the language, know the history, or understand the politics. You just need to eat.

## The Food Discovery Framework

### Day 1: Take a Guided Food Tour
The single best investment for a food-focused trip. A 3-4 hour food tour on your first full day teaches you:
- What the local specialties are and where to find them
- How to navigate a market or food street
- What to look for in quality (freshness signals, busy vs. empty stalls)
- Neighborhoods you wouldn't have found on your own
- A local's perspective on what's authentic vs. tourist trap

### Days 2+: Apply What You Learned
After the tour, you have a framework. Now:
- Revisit the market on your own
- Try the vendors the guide mentioned
- Ask your accommodation host for their personal favorites
- Follow construction workers, taxi drivers, and office workers at lunch -- they know where the value is

## Street Food Heuristics

### Safety Assessment (not fear, just awareness)
- **High turnover = fresh**: If a stall has a line of locals, the food is being made constantly. That's your safest bet.
- **Cooked to order**: Watch it being made in front of you. Heat kills bacteria.
- **Avoid pre-made and sitting**: Food that's been sitting at room temperature is the risk, not street food itself.
- **Follow the crowds**: Empty stalls aren't empty because the food is bad -- but a packed stall is a strong positive signal.
- **Peel it, cook it, or skip it**: For raw items, stick to fruits you can peel yourself.

### Ordering Strategy
- Point at what looks good (pointing is universally understood)
- Say "same as them" or point at another customer's plate
- Start small -- one portion. If it's great, get another
- Carry small bills and coins (many street vendors can't break large notes)

## Market Exploration

### Types of Markets
| Type | Best For | Tips |
|---|---|---|
| Morning wet markets | Fresh produce, seafood, local breakfast | Go early (6-8 AM), bring cash, don't block aisles |
| Night markets | Cooked food, snacks, social atmosphere | Go hungry, share dishes, pace yourself |
| Weekend/farmer's markets | Artisan foods, seasonal produce | Best for cheese, bread, preserves, honey |
| Floating/outdoor markets | Unique experience, photogenic | Tourist-heavy but still worth it for the spectacle |
| Covered/historic markets | All-weather, varied vendors | Often have both tourist and local sections -- explore deeper |

### Market Behavior
- Walk the entire market once before buying anything
- Ask vendors "what's good today?" or "what's in season?"
- Buy small quantities of many things rather than large quantities of few
- Try before you buy when offered
- Bring your own bag

## Cooking Classes

One of the highest-value travel experiences. In 3-4 hours you:
- Learn 4-6 dishes
- Visit a market (usually included)
- Understand technique and ingredients
- Take recipes home
- Meet other travelers (social bonus)

### How to Choose a Good Class
- **Market visit included**: The shopping is half the lesson
- **Small group** (under 10 people): More hands-on time
- **Home kitchen or local chef**: More authentic than a cooking school factory
- **Hands-on, not demo**: You should be cooking, not watching
- **Recipes provided**: So you can recreate at home

### Best Regions for Cooking Classes
Every food culture has them, but these are especially developed: Thailand, Italy, Japan, India, Mexico, Morocco, Peru, Greece, Vietnam

## Dining Frameworks

### The "Three Tiers" Approach
For each destination, plan meals across three tiers:
1. **Street/market food**: 60% of meals. Cheap, authentic, adventurous
2. **Local restaurants**: 30% of meals. Sit-down, neighborhood spots, moderate price
3. **Special occasion**: 10% of meals. One or two memorable dinners. Worth the splurge

### Finding Real Local Restaurants
- **Walk 3+ blocks from any tourist attraction**: The restaurants get better and cheaper with every block
- **Look for local-language menus**: If the menu is only in English, it's for tourists
- **Check the clientele**: Mostly locals = good sign. Mostly tourists = not necessarily bad, but worth asking why
- **Lunchtime is the insider move**: Many restaurants serve the same quality food at lunch for 40-60% of the dinner price
- **Ask specific questions**: Don't ask "where should I eat?" Ask "where do YOU eat when you want [specific dish]?"

### Solo Dining
- **Sit at the bar/counter**: Better service, conversation with staff, no awkward empty chair
- **Go at off-peak times**: 11:30 lunch, 6:00 dinner. Less crowded, more attention from staff
- **Bring a book**: It's your permission slip to take your time
- **Embrace it**: Some of the best meals happen alone. You pay more attention to the food.

## Regional Food Research

Before arrival, research:
- **The 5 must-try dishes**: Every region has them. Know their names.
- **The local drink**: Beer, wine, spirit, tea, coffee -- what's the thing here?
- **Meal timing**: When do locals eat? (Spain: dinner at 10 PM. Japan: dinner at 6-7 PM.)
- **Breakfast culture**: Is breakfast a meal or an afterthought? What do locals actually eat?
- **Seasonal specialties**: What's only available right now?

## Dietary Restrictions Abroad

### Preparation
- Learn how to say your restriction in the local language
- Carry a printed card explaining your allergy/restriction in the local language (multiple apps/websites generate these)
- Research the cuisine for naturally compatible dishes (many Asian cuisines are naturally gluten-free with rice-based dishes; Mediterranean cuisine has abundant vegetarian options)

### Common Challenges
| Restriction | Easier Destinations | Harder Destinations |
|---|---|---|
| Vegetarian | India, Thailand, Italy | Argentina, Mongolia, rural anywhere |
| Vegan | Thailand, Israel, Germany | France, Japan (dashi in everything) |
| Gluten-free | SE Asia, Mexico | Italy, France, Middle East |
| Halal/Kosher | Muslim-majority countries, Israel | Remote areas, countries without significant communities |
| Nut allergy | Japan, Scandinavia | SE Asia, Middle East, India |

### The Reality Check
In some destinations, strict dietary restrictions are genuinely difficult. Research honestly. Sometimes the right answer is to relax restrictions slightly, and sometimes it's to choose a different destination. Neither is wrong.

## Bringing It Home

The best souvenir is a recipe you can cook:
- Buy spices and specialty ingredients (check customs rules)
- Take photos of ingredient labels and market stalls
- Write down recipes immediately (you'll forget by next week)
- Host a dinner party within 2 weeks of returning (before the motivation fades)
