# Experience Type Taxonomy

Every trip is a blend of experience types. Understanding what draws you helps choose destinations, plan days, and allocate budget. Rate your interest in each (skip, light touch, or deep focus).

## The Spectrum

### Adventure & Physical
**Energy**: High | **Social**: Variable | **Budget**: Moderate-High

Activities: trekking, rock climbing, kayaking, surfing, diving, caving, canyoning, mountain biking, paragliding, skiing, zip-lining, white-water rafting

**Best for**: People who feel rested after physical exertion, who want to earn their views, who get restless sitting on a beach.

**Planning notes**: Requires fitness-appropriate matching. Book guides for technical activities. Weather windows matter critically. Gear rental vs. bring-your-own decision. Always have a rest day after high-intensity days.

---

### Relaxation & Restoration
**Energy**: Low | **Social**: Low-Medium | **Budget**: Variable

Activities: beach days, pool time, reading, slow walks, naps, spa visits, sunset watching, cafe sitting, journaling, hammock time

**Best for**: Burnout recovery, couples reconnecting, people who normally run at 100% and need permission to do nothing.

**Planning notes**: Resist the urge to "optimize" relaxation. Don't schedule a spa visit between two museum visits. Whole days of nothing are the point. The hardest part is fighting the guilt of "wasting" a travel day.

---

### Cultural Immersion
**Energy**: Medium | **Social**: High | **Budget**: Low-Medium

Activities: heritage sites, museums, guided walking tours, religious ceremonies (respectfully), local festivals, language exchange, homestays, volunteering (ethical), traditional craft workshops

**Best for**: Curious minds, repeat travelers bored of surface-level tourism, people who read about a place before visiting.

**Planning notes**: Depth over breadth. One museum experienced fully beats three rushed. Hire local guides -- they transform sites from "old building" to "story." Budget time for processing (journal, discuss, sit and think). See cultural-preparation.md for doing this respectfully.

---

### Culinary Exploration
**Energy**: Low-Medium | **Social**: Medium-High | **Budget**: Variable (street food to fine dining)

Activities: market tours, cooking classes, food walks, wine/beer/spirits tastings, farm visits, foraging, restaurant meals as events, street food missions, food festival attendance

**Best for**: Anyone who considers food a core part of culture (which it is). People who remember trips by what they ate. Those who want to bring skills home.

**Planning notes**: See culinary-travel.md for full framework. The single best investment for a food trip is one guided food tour early in the trip -- it teaches you how to find good food for the remaining days.

---

### Nature & Wildlife
**Energy**: Variable | **Social**: Low-Medium | **Budget**: Low-High

Activities: national park exploration, wildlife safaris, birdwatching, whale watching, snorkeling/diving reef systems, stargazing, botanical gardens, forest bathing, volcano visits, desert expeditions

**Best for**: People who recharge outdoors, families with children (nature is the great equalizer for mixed-age groups), photographers.

**Planning notes**: See nature-wildlife.md. Season matters enormously (migration windows, bloom periods, water levels). Permits often required weeks/months in advance. "Leave No Trace" is non-negotiable. Guided vs. self-guided depends on remoteness and your experience level.

---

### Urban Exploration
**Energy**: Medium-High | **Social**: High | **Budget**: Variable

Activities: neighborhood walks, architecture tours, street art hunting, coffee shop hopping, rooftop views, public transit adventures, people watching, bookshop browsing, record store digging, vintage/thrift shopping, nightlife

**Best for**: People energized by density and variety, first-time international travelers (cities have more tourist infrastructure), introverts who enjoy observing.

**Planning notes**: Organize by neighborhood, not by attraction. Spend a full morning in one area rather than crisscrossing the city. Use public transit as sightseeing. The best urban experiences are often unplanned -- leave gaps. Walk more than you think you should.

---

### Wellness & Retreat
**Energy**: Low | **Social**: Low | **Budget**: Medium-High

Activities: yoga retreats, meditation sits, thermal baths, spa programs, digital detox, silent retreats, breathwork, sound healing, forest bathing, tai chi, health-focused cooking

**Best for**: Life transitions, burnout recovery, people who want to return genuinely changed rather than just rested. See wellness-travel.md.

**Planning notes**: Research thoroughly -- quality varies enormously. Check teacher/practitioner credentials. Understand what you're signing up for (a 10-day silent Vipassana is not a spa weekend). Some wellness travel is genuinely transformative; some is expensive nonsense. The difference is usually in the facilitators, not the facilities.

---

### Creative & Learning
**Energy**: Medium | **Social**: Medium | **Budget**: Low-Medium

Activities: photography workshops, writing retreats, painting/sketching, pottery/ceramics, weaving, language immersion schools, dance classes, music lessons, artisan apprenticeships, architecture tours

**Best for**: People who travel to create, not just consume. Those who want to bring back a skill, not just a souvenir.

**Planning notes**: The "student" posture opens doors that "tourist" doesn't. Taking even a single class creates a different relationship with a place. Multi-day workshops provide built-in community. Don't over-schedule creative activities -- they need processing time.

---

### Historical & Archaeological
**Energy**: Medium | **Social**: Low-Medium | **Budget**: Low-Medium

Activities: ancient ruins, archaeological sites, historical walking tours, war memorials and battlefields, genealogy travel, living history museums, archive visits, historical reenactments

**Best for**: History enthusiasts, people connecting with ancestral heritage, those who want context for the present.

**Planning notes**: Read at least one book about the period/place before going. A great guide is worth 10x the cost. Go early (most sites are empty at opening, packed by noon). Budget time to sit and absorb, not just photograph.

---

## Blending Experience Types

Most great trips blend 2-3 types. Common pairings:

| Primary | Natural Complement | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Adventure | Nature | Trekking in Patagonia with wildlife detours |
| Cultural | Culinary | Japanese temple visits + ramen tours + cooking class |
| Relaxation | Wellness | Beach resort with daily yoga and spa |
| Urban | Culinary | European city trip organized around food neighborhoods |
| Nature | Creative | Photography-focused national park trip |
| Historical | Cultural | Walking ancient trade routes with local guides |

## The "One Thing Per Trip" Principle

The most memorable trips often have a single defining experience that everything else orbits:
- "The trip where we hiked to Machu Picchu"
- "The trip where we learned to make pasta in Bologna"
- "The trip where we did nothing but read on the beach"

Ask: "If this trip could only be known for one thing, what would it be?" Plan around that anchor.
