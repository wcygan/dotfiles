# Health & Safety Preparation

Health prep is the most neglected and potentially consequential aspect of trip planning. Do it early -- some preparations need weeks of lead time.

## Pre-Trip Health Timeline

### 8+ Weeks Before Departure
- **Vaccinations**: Check CDC (US) or equivalent for destination requirements. Some vaccines need multiple doses over weeks
- **Prescription medications**: Get enough for the trip plus 7 extra days. Carry doctor's note/prescription in your name. Research legality at destination (some common medications are controlled in other countries)
- **Travel health consultation**: For exotic destinations, see a travel medicine specialist. Worth the $50-100 appointment
- **Dental check**: Get dental work done before you leave. Dental emergencies abroad are expensive and stressful
- **Travel insurance**: Purchase now (see section below)

### 2-4 Weeks Before
- **Malaria prophylaxis**: If needed, start before departure (timing varies by medication)
- **Copies of prescriptions**: Digital and printed. Include generic drug names (brand names differ by country)
- **Research nearest hospitals/clinics**: Know where to go before you need to
- **First aid kit**: Assemble or purchase (see below)

### 1 Week Before
- **Check health advisories**: Any disease outbreaks or warnings for your destination?
- **Jet lag prep**: For 5+ timezone crossings, begin shifting sleep schedule 2-3 days early

## Travel Insurance

### Why It's Non-Negotiable
A medical evacuation from a remote area can cost $50,000-$200,000+. A broken bone treated abroad can cost thousands. Travel insurance typically costs 4-8% of your trip cost and covers:
- Emergency medical treatment
- Medical evacuation
- Trip cancellation/interruption
- Lost/delayed luggage
- Personal liability

### Evaluation Criteria
- **Medical coverage limit**: Minimum $100,000 for international travel. $250,000+ for adventure activities or remote destinations
- **Evacuation coverage**: Included? What's the limit?
- **Pre-existing conditions**: Covered or excluded? Read the fine print
- **Adventure sports**: Standard policies often exclude diving, trekking above certain altitudes, skiing, etc. Get a policy that covers your planned activities
- **Deductible**: Higher deductible = lower premium, but you pay more for small claims

### What Your Credit Card May Already Cover
Many travel credit cards include:
- Trip cancellation insurance
- Lost luggage coverage
- Rental car collision damage waiver
- Travel accident insurance

Check your card benefits before buying duplicate coverage. But note: credit card coverage is often secondary (pays after your primary insurance) and has significant exclusions.

## Jet Lag Management

### How It Works
Your body clock shifts approximately 1-1.5 hours per day. A 6-timezone crossing takes 4-6 days to fully adjust.

### The Light Exposure Protocol
- **Traveling east**: Seek morning light, avoid evening light
- **Traveling west**: Seek evening light, avoid morning light
- **Why**: Light is the strongest signal for resetting your circadian rhythm

### Practical Strategies
- **Pre-adjust 2-3 days before**: Shift bedtime 1 hour per day toward the destination time
- **On the plane**: Set your watch to destination time immediately. Eat and sleep according to destination schedule
- **First day**: Resist napping until local bedtime. If you must nap, set an alarm for 20-30 minutes
- **Melatonin**: 0.5-3mg taken 30 minutes before desired bedtime at the destination. Can help reset the clock (consult your doctor)
- **Caffeine strategy**: Use strategically to stay awake. Cut off 6 hours before desired bedtime
- **Exercise**: Morning exercise at the destination helps reset your rhythm

### Direction Matters
- **Eastbound is harder**: You're shortening your day. More jet lag, slower adjustment
- **Westbound is easier**: You're lengthening your day. Most people adjust faster

## Altitude Considerations

### The Risk Zones
| Altitude | Risk Level | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 0-2,500m (8,200ft) | Minimal | Most people unaffected |
| 2,500-3,500m | Moderate | Symptoms possible. Go slow on day 1 |
| 3,500-5,500m | High | Acclimatization protocol required |
| 5,500m+ | Very high | Experienced mountaineers only |

### Acclimatization Protocol
- **Ascend gradually**: Above 3,000m, gain no more than 300-500m sleeping altitude per day
- **Climb high, sleep low**: Day hikes to higher altitude, return to sleep at lower altitude
- **Hydrate aggressively**: Drink more water than you think you need
- **Know the symptoms**: Headache, nausea, fatigue, dizziness, loss of appetite. Mild symptoms are normal above 3,000m
- **The rule**: If symptoms worsen, stop ascending. If symptoms become severe, descend immediately

### Cities at Altitude
Some popular destinations are already high:
- Cusco, Peru: 3,400m -- plan 1-2 acclimatization days before Machu Picchu
- La Paz, Bolivia: 3,640m -- take it very easy the first day
- Lhasa, Tibet: 3,650m -- acclimatization required
- Bogota, Colombia: 2,640m -- mild effects for some
- Mexico City: 2,240m -- usually no issues

## First Aid Kit Essentials

Keep it small. You're not a hospital -- you're covering the gap until you can reach one.

### The Basics
- Pain reliever (ibuprofen or acetaminophen)
- Anti-diarrheal (loperamide)
- Antihistamine (for allergic reactions)
- Rehydration salts (for diarrhea or heat exhaustion)
- Band-aids, small gauze pads, medical tape
- Antiseptic wipes or small bottle of antiseptic
- Tweezers (splinters, ticks)
- Blister treatment (moleskin or blister patches)
- Sunscreen (SPF 30+)
- Insect repellent (DEET-based for malaria/dengue areas)

### For Specific Trips
- **Tropical**: Anti-malarial medication (if prescribed), stronger insect repellent, water purification tablets
- **High altitude**: Acetazolamide (Diamox) -- prescription required, discuss with doctor before
- **Adventure/remote**: SAM splint, compression bandage, emergency whistle, waterproof matches

## Safety Awareness

### General Principles
- **Situational awareness**: Know what's normal for the environment. If something feels off, trust that feeling
- **Valuables**: Hotel safe for passport and spare cash. Carry a photocopy. Money belt or hidden pouch for daily cash in high-risk areas
- **Phone**: Keep it charged, have offline maps downloaded, know emergency numbers
- **Scam awareness**: Research common scams for your destination. Most are avoidable once you know the pattern

### Common Scam Patterns (worldwide)
- **The "helpful" stranger**: Unsolicited help with directions/tickets, then demands payment
- **The broken taxi meter**: "It's broken, I'll give you a good price" (it won't be)
- **The friendship bracelet/flower**: Someone ties a bracelet or hands you a flower, then demands money
- **The petition signer**: Distraction technique while a partner pickpockets
- **The closed attraction**: "That temple/museum is closed today, but I know a great shop..."

### Response Framework
- Be polite but firm: "No, thank you" is a complete sentence
- Walk away. Keep walking. Don't engage in negotiation
- If something seems too good to be true (free tour, incredible deal), it is
- Use official taxi stands, ride-hailing apps, or pre-arranged transport
- Ask your accommodation for current safety advice -- they know the area

## Emergency Preparation

### Before You Leave
- Register with your country's embassy/consulate at the destination
- Share your itinerary with someone at home
- Save emergency numbers in your phone AND written on paper:
  - Local emergency number (not always 911)
  - Your embassy/consulate
  - Travel insurance emergency line
  - Your bank's international collect-call number (for lost cards)
  - A trusted contact at home

### Digital Backup
- Photos of passport, visa, insurance card, prescriptions in cloud storage
- Offline maps of your destination downloaded
- Translation app with offline language pack
- Emergency contacts accessible without internet
