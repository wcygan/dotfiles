# Itinerary Design Frameworks

The difference between a great trip and an exhausting one is usually not where you go -- it's how you pace it.

## Planning Philosophies

### The "Anchor and Wander" Method
Plan ONE anchor activity per day. Everything else is flexible.

- Morning: anchor (museum visit, guided tour, hike)
- Afternoon: wander (explore the neighborhood, follow curiosity)
- Evening: emerge naturally (dinner reservation or street food, depending on mood)

**Best for**: First-time visitors, cultural trips, urban exploration.

### The "Neighborhood Immersion" Approach
Instead of planning by attraction, plan by area. Spend a full half-day or day in one neighborhood.

- No crisscrossing the city
- Walk everywhere within the zone
- Eat where the locals eat in that area
- Discover what's there, don't just hit the highlights

**Best for**: Repeat visitors, food-focused trips, walkable cities.

### The "3-3-1" Rhythm (for 7-day trips)
- **3 days exploring**: Active, outward-facing, new things
- **3 days relaxing**: Slow mornings, revisit favorites, rest
- **1 day wildcard**: Completely unplanned, follow the mood

Adjust ratio for longer trips, but maintain the rhythm. Humans need oscillation, not sustained intensity.

### The "Leave Gaps" Principle
Never schedule more than 60-70% of available time. The remaining 30-40% is for:
- Serendipity (the best travel moments are unplanned)
- Recovery (you're more tired than you think)
- Discovery (that side street, that local recommendation, that unexpected festival)
- Flexibility (rain, illness, changed plans, extending something great)

## Day Structure Templates

### The Active Explorer Day
```
07:00  Wake, coffee, walk neighborhood
08:30  Breakfast (local spot, not hotel)
09:30  Main activity (museum, hike, tour) - 2-3 hours
12:30  Lunch near activity location
14:00  Wander / secondary interest / rest
16:00  Cafe break or afternoon rest
17:30  Golden hour walk / viewpoint
19:00  Dinner
21:00  Evening activity or wind down
```

### The Slow Day
```
        Wake naturally
        Long breakfast / brunch
        Read, journal, or light walk
        Lunch
        One gentle activity (market, park, cafe)
        Nap or pool time
        Sunset
        Dinner at a place you've been wanting to try
```

### The Travel/Transit Day
```
        Pack and check out
        Transit (THIS IS THE WHOLE DAY)
        Arrive, check in, orient
        Walk the immediate neighborhood (15 min)
        Simple dinner nearby
        Early sleep
        DO NOT plan anything else on this day
```

## Multi-Destination Trip Design

### Hub-and-Spoke
One base, day trips outward. Unpack once.
- **Pro**: No packing/unpacking, deep knowledge of one place, flexible day trips
- **Con**: Limited range, transit time to spokes
- **Best for**: Regions with good transit (European cities, Japanese rail hubs)

### Linear Route
A to B to C to D, no backtracking.
- **Pro**: Maximum variety, sense of journey
- **Con**: More packing/unpacking, can't extend a favorite place without derailing the sequence
- **Best for**: Road trips, train journeys, when places are naturally sequential

### Loop Route
A to B to C back to A. Start and end at the same point.
- **Pro**: Can book round-trip transport, revisit favorites on the way back
- **Con**: Backtracking on the return leg
- **Best for**: When flying in/out of the same airport is cheapest, or when you want a "bookend" experience

### The "Base + One" Model
Spend 70% of the trip in one place, take one side trip.
- **Pro**: Depth + variety. You become a temporary local AND see somewhere new.
- **Best for**: 7-14 day trips where one destination is the clear draw

## Pacing Rules

### The Day Zero Rule
Your arrival day is not a sightseeing day. Don't plan activities on:
- The day you fly in (especially overnight flights or long-haul)
- The day you change cities (transit + check-in + orient = full day)

### The Day Minus-One Rule
Your departure day is a logistics day. Plan backwards:
- When is your flight/train?
- When do you need to leave for the airport/station?
- When do you need to check out?
- Everything before that is packing, last-minute shopping, and one final coffee

### The Rest Day Cadence

| Trip Length | Recommended Rest Days | Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| 3-4 days | 0-1 | Keep it light on the last day |
| 5-7 days | 1-2 | Day 3 or 4 is usually the wall |
| 8-14 days | 2-3 | Every 3-4 active days, take one slow |
| 15-30 days | 4-7 | Weekly rhythm: 5 active, 2 slow |
| 30+ days | As needed | You'll naturally slow down. Listen to it |

### The Energy Alternation Pattern
Don't stack high-energy days:
- High-energy day (long hike, city walk, full museum day)
- Medium-energy day (cooking class, neighborhood exploration, guided tour)
- Low-energy day (beach, spa, cafe hopping, market browsing)

### The Front-Load Flexibility Rule
Put your "nice to do" activities early. Put your "must do" activities in the second half.

**Why**: By the second half, you'll know what matters to you in this place. You might discover something early that's worth extending. And if you front-load must-dos and then discover something unexpected, you have no room.

## Trip Duration Frameworks

| Duration | What's Realistic | Example Shape |
|---|---|---|
| 3-4 days (long weekend) | One city or small area, deep | 3 days in one neighborhood |
| 5-7 days (one week) | One city + day trip, or two cities | 5 days in Barcelona + 2 in a coastal town |
| 8-14 days (two weeks) | One country, 2-3 areas | 5 days Tokyo, 4 days Kyoto, 3 days rural area |
| 15-21 days (three weeks) | One country deeply or 2 countries | Or one slow circumnavigation of a region |
| 30+ days | One region, many stops, slow pace | SE Asia backpack, European rail, road trip |

### Cities Per Trip (Maximum)

A controversial but useful rule of thumb:

| Duration | Max Cities/Bases | Why |
|---|---|---|
| 5 days | 1 | Seriously. One city in 5 days is perfect |
| 7 days | 1-2 | If the second is very close (train < 2 hours) |
| 10 days | 2-3 | Two bases with one day trip |
| 14 days | 3-4 | But consider 2 bases with side trips instead |
| 21+ days | Still not more than 5 | Moving is exhausting. Depth beats breadth |

## When Plans Should Change

Good reasons to deviate from the itinerary:
- Weather makes an outdoor activity miserable or an indoor one appealing
- A local recommends something you hadn't considered
- You discover you love a place and want to stay longer
- You're exhausted and need a rest day NOW, not in two days
- A festival or event is happening nearby

Bad reasons to deviate:
- FOMO about something you read online that morning
- Guilt about not "seeing enough"
- Someone on social media just posted from a place nearby
