# Trip Archetypes

The composition of your travel group shapes every other decision. Each archetype has distinct strengths, pitfalls, and planning considerations.

## Solo Travel

**Superpower**: Total freedom. You eat when you're hungry, move when you're restless, change plans without negotiation.

**Common pitfalls**:
- Over-scheduling to avoid loneliness (leave room for spontaneous connection)
- Not telling anyone your rough itinerary (share it with someone at home)
- Decision fatigue (pre-decide a few things: first night dinner, morning routine, one anchor activity per day)

**Planning considerations**:
- Accommodation choice matters more: hostels for social connection, hotels/Airbnb for recharging
- Table-for-one anxiety fades after the second meal -- lean into it
- Solo travel is not inherently more dangerous, but requires more situational awareness
- Best for: first 3-5 trips to build confidence, destinations with strong solo-traveler infrastructure (SE Asia, Europe, Japan, New Zealand)

**Safety framework**:
- Share live location with a trusted person
- Keep digital copies of all documents in cloud storage
- Research emergency numbers and nearest embassy before arrival
- Trust your instincts -- if something feels off, leave

## Couple Travel

**Superpower**: Shared memory-making, mutual support, splitting costs.

**Common pitfalls**:
- Assuming your partner wants the same trip you do (ASK, explicitly, early)
- Planning everything together, doing everything together -- build in solo time
- The "compromise trip" where nobody gets what they want
- Stress-testing the relationship with too much togetherness + logistical friction

**Planning considerations**:
- Have the "what's your dream day" conversation separately, then compare
- Build in at least one half-day apart per week ("you go to the museum, I'll go to the market")
- Alternate who plans each day (ownership reduces conflict)
- Budget conversation must happen before the trip, not during
- At least one "splurge" experience you both care about

**The alignment framework**:
- List each person's top 3 priorities independently
- Find overlaps (these are your anchors)
- Non-overlapping items get dedicated solo time
- Agree on daily rhythm: are you both early risers? Does one need more downtime?

## Family Travel (with kids)

**Superpower**: Kids experience everything with fresh wonder. Travel shapes who they become.

**Common pitfalls**:
- Planning an adult trip and adding kids to it (plan FOR the kids, adults get enriched too)
- Over-scheduling (kids need downtime 2x more than adults)
- Ignoring meal timing (hangry kids = ruined afternoon)
- Underestimating transit time with small children (everything takes 1.5x longer)

**Planning considerations by age**:

| Age | Key Need | Planning Impact |
|---|---|---|
| 0-2 | Routine, nap schedule, safe spaces | Short activity windows, accommodation with kitchen, pack familiar items |
| 3-6 | Physical activity, snacks, sensory engagement | Playgrounds, beaches, animal encounters, frequent breaks, early bedtimes |
| 7-12 | Stimulation, participation, "being included" | Interactive museums, cooking classes, treasure hunts, assign them a "job" (navigator, photographer) |
| 13-17 | Autonomy, social media moments, not being embarrassed | Give them free time in safe areas, let them pick one activity per day, separate hotel room if budget allows |

**The "one for them, one for us" pattern**: Alternate kid-focused activities (playground, beach, zoo) with adult-interest activities where kids can participate (walking tour, market, cafe culture).

**Packing multiplier**: Every item you'd normally pack, multiply effort by 1.5x for each child. But kids are more adaptable than parents think -- you don't need to bring their entire bedroom.

## Friend Group Travel

**Superpower**: Shared costs, social energy, more adventurous choices.

**Common pitfalls**:
- The "democracy trap": group voting on every meal and activity = lowest common denominator
- Unspoken budget differences (one person's "reasonable dinner" is another's "that's outrageous")
- The organizer doing all the work and resenting it
- Never splitting up (6 people at dinner is fun; 6 people at every activity is a tour group)

**Planning considerations**:
- **Budget conversation first**: "What's your total trip budget?" before any planning
- **Designate roles**: one person books accommodation, another plans restaurants, another handles logistics
- **The "split and reconvene" model**: travel together, have shared meals, but do activities in subgroups by interest
- **One group activity per day max**: leave the rest flexible
- **Expense tracking**: agree on a system before the trip (Splitwise, shared spreadsheet, or take turns paying)
- **The "veto, not vote" rule**: anyone can veto something they truly hate, but don't vote on everything

## Workation

**Superpower**: Extended stay without burning all PTO. Work in the morning, explore in the afternoon.

**Common pitfalls**:
- Working more than you planned because the boundary is blurry
- Choosing destinations based on Instagram, not wifi reliability
- Timezone misalignment causing 6 AM or 11 PM meetings
- Never fully being "on vacation" or "at work" -- stuck in limbo

**Planning considerations**:
- **Non-negotiable**: reliable wifi, a dedicated work space (not a bed), timezone overlap with team
- **Schedule**: set fixed work hours and honor them. 8 AM to 1 PM work, rest of day is yours
- **Duration sweet spot**: 2-4 weeks. Less than 2 weeks isn't worth the setup cost. More than 4 weeks and you stop exploring
- **Accommodation**: Airbnb/apartment over hotel. You need a desk, kitchen, and quiet
- **Communication**: tell your team you're doing this. Don't hide it
- **Best destinations**: places where your work hours map to quiet morning + active afternoon

## Sabbatical / Extended Travel (1-6 months)

**Superpower**: Depth. You stop being a tourist and start being a temporary resident.

**Common pitfalls**:
- Planning the whole thing in advance (over-planning kills a 3-month trip)
- Moving too fast (the backpacker trap: new city every 3 days for 3 months)
- Not budgeting for boredom (it's normal and necessary -- don't panic-plan)
- Relationship strain if you have a partner at home

**Planning considerations**:
- **Plan the first 2-3 weeks only**. Book the first few nights. Let the rest emerge
- **Slow down**: 1-2 weeks per location minimum. You learn a place on day 4, not day 1
- **Budget for the long haul**: track spending weekly, adjust monthly. Long-term stays are much cheaper per-day than short trips
- **Stay connected**: schedule regular calls home. Loneliness is real even when you're having the time of your life
- **Have a re-entry plan**: see reentry-integration.md. Coming home from a long trip is harder than people expect

## The Retreat / Solo Wellness Trip

**Superpower**: Intentional disconnection and self-focus.

**Common pitfalls**:
- Choosing a retreat to escape problems (they'll be there when you return)
- Underestimating the intensity (a 10-day silent retreat is not a spa vacation)
- Not researching the facilitators (credentials matter)

**Planning considerations**:
- See wellness-travel.md for detailed guidance
- Tell people where you'll be and when you'll be unreachable
- Build in buffer days before and after (don't fly home from a meditation retreat and go to work the next day)
- Be honest about what you're looking for: relaxation, transformation, or escape? They require different settings

## Choosing Your Archetype

Ask yourself:
1. Who is coming, and what do they need from this trip?
2. What's the social energy level? (solo recharge vs. group bonding vs. partner reconnection)
3. How much logistical complexity can you handle right now?
4. Is this trip about freedom (solo/couple) or connection (group/family)?

Your archetype determines accommodation style, itinerary density, budget allocation, and the fundamental rhythm of each day.
